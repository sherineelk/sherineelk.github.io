Review of Getty's website on the Disney Concert Hall

Visit Getty's Website

What was the first thing you paid attention to when interacting with the experience?

I first paid attention to classical music playing in the background and how the lines being drawn in the background while the website loads.

Spend two minutes with the experience and create a list of each of your discrete actions.

  • - Scrolling down towards the introduction of the concert hall
  • - scrolling down further to advance the scenic footage of the concert hall
  • - clicking to transition to the video segment
  • - Further scrolling down through the text segment of the ‘chapter’

What part of the experience did you spend the most time engaging with?

It would be the small video sections where Frank Gehry was explaining the process behind certain sections of the building. I felt somewhat discouraged to leave from the video section as there was a small text pop informing me if I were to scroll further I would exit the videos.

What was the most common action in your two minute interaction with the experience?

It would most likely be scrolling down as a lot of the site’s function was tied to that action, such as transitioning between each chapter or segment of the website, or from final product to sketch.

What is your impression of the intended primary goal of the interactive experience?

To provide a glimpse into the thought process and the effort Frank Gehry's teams took to design the concert hall, in a grand, refined yet welcoming manner.

How does the interactive experience communicate this primary goal?

The choice of classic music and smooth sweeping transitions between the different chapters adds this feeling of refinement. Additionally The scenic footage at the top of the webiste showcasing the scale and different angles of the building communicates a sense of grandeur to the viewer. This is well contrasted against the building sketches, large blocks of colour and thick typeface which give more of a raw feeling, as if the viewer is given a glimpse into the workspace where the design process of this building took place. These two main themes are combined in the header of each chapter, as pictures of different sections of the concert hall sweeped over by the initial sketches. This also hints to the viewer what Gehry will be talking about next in the video section.

What is your impression of how the experience should be interacted with over time? (For how long and how many different times)

The experience is most likely intended to be interacted with only once for a short duration of time, at most 10 minutes.

How does the interactive experience communicate how it should be interacted with over time?

The overall layout of the experience promotes a fast pace viewing. In the text section with the text placed in a zigzag-like manner, with pictures taking up a little over half the layout, more information would be hidden underneath a ‘read more’ button that links to another page. The videos are broken down into 4 small clips that can be skipped through, rather than a longer video with multiple sections.

What other media forms (digital or otherwise) does the experience reference?

The initial sketches and large blocks of color that make up the chapter divisions reminds me of architecture drawings on colour construction paper. The introduction section that involves b-roll of the concert hall is shot similar to a movie or documentary. The text sections are reminiscent of a design magazine that puts more emphasis to wards the picture, rather than the writing. The video section layout mimics the short form content seen on social media. Towards the bottom of the website the index is laid out similar to an art musuem's online archive.

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should act when engaging with your research experience?

The text section gives me the impression to skim over it as I scroll through it,similar to how I would act when flipping through a paper magazine. The video section gives me the idea that I’m supposed to swipe down like I’m on YouTube shorts, however this can only done through clicking right to go to the next clip. At the index that I felt like I should slowly appreciate the models and sketches provided like I’m at a museum.

What does this reference/s communicate to you about how you should feel when engaging with your research experience?

The introduction scene and text sections did impress me and added a sense of ethos when interacting with the website, as if I could trust what i was reading. The video section did give me a sense of familiarity as I'm personally more used to short form content, thus the design explaination seemed less intimidating and easier to process. The index made me stop and take in the models and sketches from a more objective point of view, as there's less narrative force driving this section when compared to the others.

What is the most frustrating part of the interaction to you and what makes that part frustrating?

The video section, while informative, feels static compared to the rest of the website. Having to scroll past the videos and having to reconfirm that I’m leaving to the next page tampers and slows down the normally dynamic but fun experience.

What is the most satisfying part of the interaction to you and what makes that part satisfying?

It would be scrolling down in general. The action is overall satisfying for a variety of reasons. For example, the subtle movements of the pictures as you scroll through the explanation section, and the sweeping transitions between the sketches or models to the fully constructed concert hall in all of its glory, It made me scroll up and down multiple times in different segments to experience the transformation again.
